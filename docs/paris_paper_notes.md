# Notes from the Paris 15-Minute City Paper

Paper:

Barthelemy, M. (2026). Why urban heterogeneity limits the 15-minute city. arXiv:2603.12122v2. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2603.12122

## Core Argument

The paper argues that the 15-minute city has structural limits when applied to commuting and employment. The key issue is urban heterogeneity: large employers cannot always be distributed locally in a way that lets every worker reach their job within 15 minutes. Even under optimized placement, a few very large firms need workers from large catchment areas.

The paper models firm sizes with a Zipf distribution. When employment is highly concentrated in large firms, the minimum possible commute time has a lower bound determined by the city's spatial scale and economic structure. In the Paris case, the author finds that universal 15-minute commuting would require deep economic restructuring, not only better transport or better spatial planning.

## What This Means for Our Shanghai Project

This project should not claim that every urban function can be made 15-minute accessible. The Paris paper is a useful warning: daily services and health amenities are more appropriate targets for proximity analysis than employment, because employment location is shaped by firm-size concentration and metropolitan labor markets.

For Track A, this supports three methodological choices:

1. The main score focuses on everyday health, sport, green space, healthy food, and local services.
2. Employment access is not included in the Track A score.
3. The web app should include a limitations note explaining that high local amenity access does not imply short commutes or full urban self-sufficiency.

## Transferable Method Ideas

- Treat the 15-minute city as a feasibility question, not only a mapping exercise.
- Distinguish local daily services from metropolitan-scale functions such as employment.
- Avoid presenting the 15-minute threshold as universally achievable.
- Use sensitivity tests where possible, for example comparing 10-, 15-, and 20-minute thresholds or walk vs bike access.
- Explain that transport mode matters: cycling can expand access, but it may not solve deeper spatial or economic concentration.

## Suggested Use in Literature Review

The paper can be used after the Shanghai measurement papers and equity critique. It adds a structural critique: even with good data and good design, proximity has limits when urban functions are unevenly distributed. This helps justify why the Shanghai project focuses on healthy lifestyle amenities rather than claiming to solve all dimensions of urban life.
